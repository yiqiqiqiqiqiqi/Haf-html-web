Famous Artists Timeline

A journey through the lives of artists who defined art history.

Leonardo da Vinci

Renaissance genius known for the Mona Lisa and The Last Supper.

Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519) was a polymath whose work spanned art, science, and engineering. His iconic Mona Lisa is renowned for its enigmatic expression.

Vincent van Gogh

Post-Impressionist painter famous for The Starry Night and Sunflowers.

Vincent van Gogh (1853–1890) created vibrant, emotional works despite personal struggles, influencing modern art with pieces like The Starry Night.

Pablo Picasso

Cubist pioneer who created Guernica and Les Demoiselles d'Avignon.

Pablo Picasso (1881–1973) revolutionized art with Cubism, producing groundbreaking works like Guernica that addressed social and political themes.

Claude Monet

Impressionist master renowned for Water Lilies and his garden paintings.

Claude Monet (1840–1926) captured light and atmosphere in his Impressionist works, with Water Lilies showcasing his mastery of color.

Michelangelo

Renaissance sculptor and painter known for the Sistine Chapel and David.

Michelangelo (1475–1564) created monumental works like the Sistine Chapel ceiling, blending art with profound human emotion.

Rembrandt

Dutch master known for The Night Watch and self-portraits.

Rembrandt (1606–1669) used light and shadow to create dramatic, introspective works, including The Night Watch.

Frida Kahlo

Mexican artist famous for her vivid self-portraits.

Frida Kahlo (1907–1954) expressed her pain and identity through vibrant, symbolic self-portraits, becoming a feminist icon.

Gustav Klimt

Symbolist painter known for The Kiss and golden artworks.

Gustav Klimt (1862–1918) blended symbolism and ornamentation in works like The Kiss, characterized by gold leaf and sensuality.

Mary Cassatt

Impressionist known for her paintings of motherhood.

Mary Cassatt (1844–1926) portrayed intimate family moments with soft colors, contributing significantly to Impressionism.

Edvard Munch

Expressionist famous for The Scream.

Edvard Munch (1863–1944) captured psychological intensity in The Scream, a cornerstone of Expressionism.

Henri Matisse

Fauvist leader known for vibrant colors and bold forms.

Henri Matisse (1869–1954) pioneered Fauvism, using bold colors to create dynamic compositions like The Dance.

Georgia O'Keeffe

Modernist known for her large-scale flower paintings.

Georgia O'Keeffe (1887–1986) celebrated nature with magnified floral works and desert landscapes, defining American Modernism.

Jackson Pollock

Abstract Expressionist famous for drip paintings.

Jackson Pollock (1912–1956) revolutionized art with his energetic drip techniques, embodying Abstract Expressionism.

Andy Warhol

Pop Art icon known for Campbell's Soup Cans.

Andy Warhol (1928–1987) redefined art with Pop Art, using consumer imagery in works like Campbell's Soup Cans.

Salvador Dalí

Surrealist known for The Persistence of Memory.

Salvador Dalí (1904–1989) explored the subconscious with dreamlike scenes, including melting clocks in The Persistence of Memory.

Pierre-Auguste Renoir

Impressionist known for vibrant social scenes.

Pierre-Auguste Renoir (1841–1919) depicted joyful gatherings with rich colors in works like Luncheon of the Boating Party.

Wassily Kandinsky

Abstract art pioneer with colorful compositions.

Wassily Kandinsky (1866–1944) created some of the first abstract works, using color to express emotion and spirituality.

Artemisia Gentileschi

Baroque painter known for dramatic works.

Artemisia Gentileschi (1593–1656) brought intensity and feminist perspectives to Baroque art with works like Judith Slaying Holofernes.

Paul Cézanne

Post-Impressionist who influenced modern art.

Paul Cézanne (1839–1906) bridged Impressionism and Cubism with structured compositions like The Card Players.

Johannes Vermeer

Dutch master known for Girl with a Pearl Earring.

Johannes Vermeer (1632–1675) created luminous, intimate scenes with meticulous detail, as seen in Girl with a Pearl Earring.
